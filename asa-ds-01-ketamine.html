Pharmacological Profile

Substance Ketamine
Class Dissociative Anaesthetic Arylcyclohexylamine NMDA Antagonist
Scientific Name (RS)-2-(2-Chlorophenyl)-2-(methylamino)cyclohexan-1-one
Common Names Ketamine · Special K · Ket · K · Vitamin K · Kit Kat · Super K
Primary Mechanism NMDA receptor open-channel blockade. Ketamine enters the NMDA receptor ion channel after it opens and physically occludes the pore — preventing calcium influx. This is "use-dependent" antagonism: the channel must open (glutamate must bind) before ketamine can enter. The result is broadly suppressed excitatory glutamatergic transmission throughout the central nervous system.
Secondary Effects Opioid receptor partial agonism (mu and delta). Sigma receptor agonism. Dopaminergic and monoaminergic activity at higher concentrations. The antidepressant mechanism of low-dose ketamine is an area of active clinical research, potentially involving AMPA receptor upregulation following NMDA blockade.
Half-Life 2 – 3 hours (plasma, multiple routes)
Onset Intramuscular: 2–5 min · Intranasal: 10–20 min · Oral: 20–30 min
Duration IM / Intranasal: 45–90 min (active) · Oral: 2–4 hours · After-effects: 1–4 hours
Primary Risks Respiratory Depression Incapacitation / K-Hole Bladder Toxicity (chronic) Psychological Dependence Dissociation / Immobility
Respiratory depression is dose-dependent and clinically significant — most dangerous when combined with other CNS depressants. The k-hole state (extreme dissociation, temporary unresponsiveness) creates acute vulnerability in unsupervised environments.
Interactions Alcohol (respiratory depression) Opioids (respiratory depression) Benzodiazepines Other CNS Depressants
Combination with CNS depressants — particularly alcohol and opioids — significantly amplifies respiratory depression risk. Unlike many anaesthetic interactions, this combination does not require large doses to produce dangerous effects.

HARM REDUCTION NOTES ASA-DS-01
Evidence-first · Non-judgemental · Operational
Never Alone
Ketamine can produce rapid, complete incapacitation. A k-hole — the state of extreme dissociation approaching temporary unresponsiveness — represents a period of absolute physical vulnerability. Anyone in a k-hole cannot respond to danger, communicate distress, or protect themselves. A trusted, sober person who understands what is happening should always be present.
Depressant Combinations
Ketamine combined with alcohol, opioids, or benzodiazepines can suppress breathing to dangerous levels. Each substance independently reduces respiratory drive; the combination is more than additive. This is not a risk that can be managed by experience with either substance in isolation — the interaction produces qualitatively different suppression.
Bladder Health
Ketamine-induced uropathy — chronic bladder and urinary tract damage — is a documented consequence of regular use. The mechanism is incompletely understood but involves ketamine and its metabolites causing inflammatory damage to the urinary tract. Symptoms include severe abdominal pain, frequent urination, and blood in urine. These require prompt clinical attention. The condition can be irreversible in severe cases.
Route Considerations
The route of administration substantially changes both onset speed and depth of effect. Intranasal use produces rapid onset and moderate dissociation. Intramuscular injection produces faster, deeper, and longer effects — the k-hole is far more likely at IM doses. Oral use is slower and more forgiving in onset, but redosing before effects are felt is a common cause of unexpectedly deep experiences.
Set and Setting
Ketamine's dissociative mechanism means the psychological character of the experience is highly sensitive to the immediate environment and mental state at onset. Unfamiliar, crowded, or unsafe environments substantially increase the risk of psychological distress. Disorientation, confusion, and inability to navigate are normal at moderate-to-high doses — this is pharmacological, not a sign of pathology. Being in a calm, safe space matters.
Psychological Dependence
Ketamine carries genuine psychological dependence potential, distinct from physical addiction in profile but not in consequence. A pattern of frequent use to manage emotional distress is a recognisable escalation pathway. Daily or near-daily use is associated with both worsening dissociative episodes and accelerated urinary tract damage. Frequency reduction and harm reduction services exist specifically for this pattern.
Wingmate Protocol
Watch your group. Ketamine can transition someone from functional to completely non-responsive faster than most substances. Red flags: person becomes very still and unresponsive to voice or touch, breathing becomes slow and shallow, they are in an awkward or unsafe position and cannot correct it, skin colour changes, vomiting while unresponsive (aspiration risk). Place an unresponsive person in the recovery position immediately. Get to the event medical team. Tell them what was taken and approximately when.

Why the first dissociative plate is ketamine.

Ketamine occupies a genuinely unusual position in contemporary psychoactive culture. It is simultaneously a licenced anaesthetic agent used in clinical surgery and emergency medicine, an FDA-approved antidepressant treatment (via esketamine nasal spray), and one of the most widely used recreational dissociatives at festivals and nightlife events globally. This range of contexts — clinical, therapeutic, and recreational — means the pharmacology is better documented than for most substances in this archive, while the specific risks in recreational settings remain poorly understood by most people who encounter it.

The dissociative class presents a distinct epistemic challenge. Unlike stimulants or empathogens, where effects scale relatively predictably with dose, ketamine has qualitative phase boundaries — the transition from mild dissociation to a k-hole is not merely a quantitative increase in effect but a shift in the character of the experience that most descriptions do not prepare people for. The archive's approach here is precise, evidence-based, and non-judgmental — acknowledging the clinical documentation of harm while avoiding the moralising frame that makes harm reduction information less accessible to the people who need it.

The NMDA Open-Channel Block Model was selected for this plate because it makes the most consequential pharmacological fact about ketamine visually legible: the mechanism is positional, not purely competitive. The blocker must enter the channel after it opens — which is why use-dependent antagonism creates the specific interaction risk with other CNS depressants. The fragmented glutamate wave in the plate represents blocked excitatory transmission across the cleft. The Ca²⁺ ions shown accumulating above the blocked pore represent the ionic consequence of that suppression — the substrate of ketamine's anaesthetic and dissociative effects.
