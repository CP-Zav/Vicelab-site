Research-informed visual pharmacology plate. 5-HT2A Cortical Agonism Model — Receptor Binding, Lid-Lock Mechanism, and Thalamo-Cortical Cascade.

Scientific Record

Pharmacological Profile

Class Psychedelic / Serotonergic Psychedelic Lysergamide Ergoline
Scientific Name Lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD-25)
Common Names LSD · Acid · Blotter · Tabs · Lucy · L · Window Pane · Microdot
Primary Mechanism Serotonin 5-HT2A partial agonism. LSD binds with exceptionally high affinity to 5-HT2A receptors on cortical pyramidal neurons and interneurons. This G-protein–coupled receptor's activation initiates downstream signalling cascades — primarily through Gq and phospholipase C-beta — that disrupt the thalamo-cortical gating system. This disruption of sensory filtering is understood to be the central driver of the psychedelic state: visual and perceptual distortions, enhanced pattern recognition, and alterations in the sense of self and time.
Lid Mechanism Unlike serotonin, LSD is physically trapped inside the 5-HT2A binding pocket by an extracellular loop that acts as a molecular cap. This "lid lock" mechanism slows receptor dissociation by orders of magnitude — a key reason LSD's effects persist well beyond its plasma half-life, and why a single dose can produce effects lasting 10–12 hours despite a plasma half-life of approximately 3–5 hours.
Secondary Activity 5-HT2C, 5-HT1A, and 5-HT6 partial agonism. Dopamine D1 and D2 receptor activity. Sigma-1 receptor affinity. Beta-arrestin biased signalling at 5-HT2A. These secondary targets modulate the affective and cognitive character of the experience without being the primary driver.
Half-Life 3 – 5 hours (plasma, oral route) · Subjective effects persist significantly beyond plasma clearance due to receptor lid-lock
Onset Oral / sublingual: 30–90 min
Duration Primary effects: 8–12 hours · Afterglow / residual stimulation: up to 24 hours
Active Dose Range Threshold: ~25–50 µg · Common: 75–150 µg · Strong: 150–300 µg · Active at the microgram scale — dosing errors are a serious risk
Typical Effects Perceptual: visual patterning and geometric distortions, colour enhancement, synesthesia, altered depth perception, trailing effects
Cognitive: intensified pattern recognition, altered sense of time, introspective loops, heightened emotional sensitivity
Somatic: mild stimulation, pupil dilation, jaw tension, mild increase in heart rate and body temperature
Primary Risks Psychological Distress Adulterant Substitution Dosing Errors HPPD Latent Psychiatric Conditions
The greatest acute risk is severe psychological distress — a "difficult experience" — particularly at high doses, in unfamiliar environments, or without adequate preparation. Adulterant substitution (NBOMe compounds, DOx series) is documented and carries substantially higher physiological risk than LSD itself.
Interactions Lithium (seizures — contraindicated) MAOIs SSRIs / SNRIs Tramadol Cannabis Stimulants
Lithium co-administration is associated with risk of seizures and should be treated as contraindicated. MAOIs can potentiate effects unpredictably. Cannabis commonly intensifies and extends the experience and significantly increases the risk of anxiety and panic.

HARM REDUCTION NOTES ASA-PS-01
Evidence-first · Non-judgemental · Operational
Set and Setting
No single factor determines an LSD experience more than set (your mental state, expectations, and intention going in) and setting (the physical and social environment). Choose familiar, safe, comfortable surroundings — especially for first or high-dose experiences. A crowded, loud, or chaotic environment that might be fine sober can become genuinely overwhelming. Emotional stress, unresolved anxiety, or poor sleep in the days before significantly increase the likelihood of a difficult experience.
Test Your Supply — Ehrlich Reagent
Substitution of LSD with NBOMe compounds (25I-NBOMe, 25C-NBOMe) and DOx-series psychedelics is documented. Both classes are active at similarly low doses but carry substantially higher physiological risk, including vasoconstriction, hyperthermia, and seizures. The Ehrlich reagent (p-DMAB) is the primary field test for LSD — it should turn purple in the presence of an indole. No colour change is a red flag. Reagent testing does not guarantee identity with certainty; confirmatory testing via laboratory services provides significantly higher confidence.
Dosing at Micrograms
LSD is active at the microgram scale — a typical blotter tab may contain anywhere from 50 to 200 µg, and the range between a threshold dose (~50 µg) and a very strong dose (200+ µg) is not visible, weighable, or detectable without laboratory analysis. Start at a known low dose. If the source is unknown, treat the first use conservatively. Effects begin 30–90 minutes after ingestion. Dosing again before the first dose has taken full effect is a common cause of unexpectedly intense experiences. HPPD (Hallucinogen Persisting Perception Disorder) risk increases with frequency and dose intensity.
Trip Sitter
For first experiences, high doses, or unfamiliar environments, a sober and trusted companion significantly reduces risk. A good trip sitter does not intervene to steer the experience unnecessarily — they provide a stable, calm presence, can help navigate if the experience becomes difficult, and are available to intervene if something goes genuinely wrong. They are not required to be a pharmacology expert. Calm, grounded presence is more valuable than technical knowledge.
Navigating Difficulty
Difficult or frightening experiences are not uncommon with LSD, particularly at higher doses or in unprepared circumstances. They are rarely dangerous physiologically — but they can be psychologically intense. Changing your physical position (sitting, lying down, moving to a quieter room), slowing your breathing, or grounding attention in a simple sensory anchor — a texture, sound, or familiar object — can help. If someone is in acute distress, stay with them, speak calmly, reassure them that the substance is temporary, and take them somewhere quieter if possible. Avoid confrontational or alarmed responses.
Medication Interactions
Lithium is contraindicated — co-administration with LSD is associated with seizure risk. If you take lithium for any reason, do not use LSD. SSRIs and SNRIs blunt psychedelic effects via 5-HT2A receptor downregulation, often leading to higher dosing attempts — increasing exposure risk. MAOIs may potentiate effects unpredictably. Tramadol carries seizure risk. If you take any regular psychiatric medication, research interactions specifically before use. Do not assume that a psychiatric prescription makes LSD safer.
Wingmate Protocol
Watch your group. LSD's duration means people may be in altered states for 10–12 hours in environments that change over that time — from comfortable to cold, from familiar to crowded. Check in on people periodically, not just at the peak. The onset is slow enough that someone may have re-dosed thinking nothing was happening. Red flags that indicate a person needs support: extreme and persistent panic despite reassurance, disorientation that is not resolving, chest pain, physical rigidity, or a person who is genuinely unresponsive. In these cases, get to the event harm reduction or medical team. Tell them what was taken and when. You will not get in trouble for doing so — this information is essential to providing appropriate care.

Why this plate exists in the archive.

LSD occupies a paradoxical position in the public record. It is simultaneously one of the most researched psychoactive substances in history — the subject of thousands of studies between the 1950s and 1970s, and again in the ongoing therapeutic research renaissance — and one of the most misrepresented in popular discourse. Neither the sensational narrative of the prohibition era nor the enthusiastic framing of therapeutic research gives festival attendees or recreational users what they operationally need.

The practical risks of LSD in nightlife and festival contexts are specific, identifiable, and largely preventable. Adulterant substitution — particularly NBOMe compounds, which produce a physically indistinguishable blotter but carry meaningfully higher physiological risk — is the most immediately dangerous. Dosing uncertainty, the slow onset that encourages redosing, and the interaction with cannabis that can rapidly escalate both intensity and anxiety are the next tier. Psychological distress is common and manageable with preparation; it is rarely a medical emergency when it occurs in conditions where basic support is available.

The 5-HT2A agonism model and the lid-lock mechanism are central to this plate because they explain two things festival-context users need to understand: why the effects are so cognitively and perceptually distinct from other substance classes, and why the duration of LSD is so much longer than its plasma half-life suggests. The expanding interference rings in the plate's visual are an abstraction of the thalamo-cortical gating disruption — the most current mechanistic account of what a psychedelic state is, at the systems level. The archive's purpose is to make that legible without requiring a neuroscience background.
