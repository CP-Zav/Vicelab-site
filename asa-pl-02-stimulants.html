Pharmacological Profile

Class Stimulant CNS Upregulant Monoamine Active
Class Members MDMA · Cocaine · Amphetamine · Methamphetamine · Cathinones · 2C-B (partial) · Caffeine
Common Names Uppers · Stims
Primary Mechanism Monoamine elevation. Most stimulants increase availability of dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin in the synaptic cleft through one or more overlapping mechanisms: blocking reuptake transporters (DAT, NET, SERT), driving reverse transport to stimulate efflux, or inhibiting monoamine oxidase (MAO). The specific mechanism varies by compound — effect profile and risk profile vary accordingly.
Downstream Effect Sustained activation of reward, arousal, and motivation circuits. Produces heightened energy, confidence, focus, and sensory intensity. Duration and depth vary significantly by compound and route of administration.
Duration Range 20 min – 12 hours depending on compound and route · Cocaine (insufflated): 45–90 min · MDMA (oral): 3–5 hours · Amphetamine (oral): 4–8 hours
Primary Risks Hyperthermia Cardiovascular Load Hyponatraemia Re-dosing Escalation Adulterants
Hyperthermia is the leading cause of stimulant-related festival deaths. Stimulant thermogenesis, physical exertion, and ambient heat accumulate multiplicatively rather than additively. Cardiovascular risk is compounded by exertion and concurrent stimulant use.
Interactions Stimulant + Stimulant MAOIs Alcohol Heat + Exertion
Combining stimulants — MDMA with cocaine, MDMA with amphetamine — multiplies cardiac and thermal load nonlinearly. MAOIs create serotonin syndrome risk with serotonergic stimulants. Heat and physical exertion are physiological interaction factors, not pharmacological ones, but functionally equivalent in their contribution to risk.

HARM REDUCTION NOTES ASA-PL-02
Evidence-first · Non-judgemental · Operational
Temperature
Take regular breaks from dancing. Seek shade or cool areas deliberately and often. Overheating is the primary mechanism of stimulant-related harm in festival environments — not overdose in the traditional clinical sense. The combination of stimulant thermogenesis, physical exertion, and ambient heat creates dangerous accumulation that is not always felt as it builds.
Hydration
For active dancing: approximately 500ml water per hour. Do not exceed this — hyponatraemia (dangerously low sodium) kills, and is caused by over-hydrating with water alone. Electrolyte drinks are preferable to plain water in extended situations. Thirst is not a reliable guide when stimulants suppress normal physiological signalling.
Dosing Behaviour
Start low. Avoid re-dosing if possible. A second dose does not restore the first experience — it extends duration and compounds cardiovascular and thermal load. The body's warning signals are pharmacologically suppressed by stimulant action. If something feels different from usual, that is an adulterant signal.
Stimulant Combinations
Stimulant combinations — particularly MDMA with cocaine or amphetamine — multiply cardiac and thermal risk nonlinearly. Each substance independently increases heart rate, blood pressure, and core temperature. Together, the load is more than additive, even at doses that would feel unremarkable used individually.
Test Before You Use
Press contamination with novel psychoactive substances (NPS) and fentanyl analogues is documented across markets. Reagent testing kits reduce — but do not eliminate — this risk. On-site testing services at events provide more reliable analysis when available. A substance that looks and feels familiar can still be substituted or adulterated.
Cardiovascular Risk
Anyone with a known cardiac condition should treat stimulant use as high-risk. Stimulants increase heart rate, blood pressure, and myocardial oxygen demand simultaneously. Exertion and heat compound this load. If experiencing chest pain, palpitations, or difficulty breathing — stop, rest, seek the medical team.
Wingmate Protocol
Watch your group. Red flags: person becomes unusually still or confused; pale, clammy, or very hot skin; stops sweating despite heat; complains of severe headache or chest pain; becomes unresponsive to voice. These require immediate access to the event medical or harm reduction team. Do not wait to see if it passes. Tell them what was taken.

Why a stimulant class plate comes before the individual substance entries.

Stimulants are the most prevalent class of psychoactive substances encountered in festival and nightlife environments. They include compounds as structurally distinct as cocaine, MDMA, amphetamine, and methamphetamine — yet these substances share enough pharmacological ground that the class-level view carries distinct harm reduction value. Understanding what all stimulants do — monoamine elevation, cardiovascular activation, thermogenesis — before drilling into specific compounds means that each subsequent substance plate can be understood not as an isolated risk profile, but as a specific variation on a shared pattern.

Most public discourse around stimulants fragments along substance lines: cocaine conversations, MDMA conversations, amphetamine conversations. This fragmentation obscures the shared risk logic that makes festival-specific guidance coherent. The archive's approach here is precise, evidence-based, and non-judgmental — the class plate is not a warning framework, it is a structural reference. The interaction risks, the thermal risks, the cardiovascular load: all of these are class properties as much as they are substance properties, and understanding them at class level is the precondition for understanding any individual entry.

The neural cascade model was chosen for this plate because it represents what the stimulant class does at its most fundamental: it amplifies signal propagation through monoamine circuits. The amber nodes and cascading paths depict dopamine and norepinephrine pathways in a state of sustained activation — not the mechanism of any one compound, but the shared signature of the class. The plate makes one thing visually legible: stimulant effects are not a switch, they are a cascade. The archive translates that pharmacological fact into a form that retains it.
