## Day 2 — Tuesday

**Platform:** TikTok (primary) / LinkedIn (adapted)
**Pillar:** Lab Notes
**Brand:** ViceLab

**Hook:**
> The reason MDMA stops working has nothing to do with tolerance.

**Full post (TikTok — 45 sec spoken):**
"Tolerance" makes it sound like your brain got used to something. That's not what's happening.

MDMA works by flooding your synapses with serotonin — your brain's reserve supply, released all at once. The problem is: that reserve takes weeks to rebuild. You're not tolerant. You're depleted.

Use again before the restock completes? You don't get the same experience. You get a body that's been asked to run dry.

This is why harm reduction advice around MDMA isn't about dosage. It's about timing. The 2–4 week gap isn't arbitrary. It's how long the supply chain takes.

Know the mechanism. The rest follows.

**Full post (LinkedIn — adapted):**
MDMA tolerance is a misframe.

What most people experience as tolerance is serotonin depletion — your brain's reserve supply released in full, requiring weeks to replenish. Use before the restock completes and the pharmacology doesn't cooperate, regardless of dose.

This distinction matters. Dosage-based advice doesn't address the actual mechanism. Timing does.

One concept, understood correctly, changes the conversation.

**Asset idea:**
TikTok: Talking head, no gimmicks. Clean background, direct eye contact, fast pace. Possibly a simple one-line text overlay at the key moment: "not tolerance. depletion."
LinkedIn: No image needed. The text carries.

**CTA:**
None. Post ends on the mechanism.

**Comments strategy:**
On TikTok: watch for comments about personal experience ("this happened to me") — reply with the specific detail that explains their experience. Don't moralize. Just extend the information. Turns individual responses into mini-Lab Notes.

## Day 3 — Wednesday

**Platform:** Instagram (primary) / LinkedIn
**Pillar:** Signal Drops
**Brand:** ViceLab

**Hook:**
> Your liver is making a third substance right now.

**Full post:**
MDMA and alcohol. Common combination. The interaction people don't know about isn't the one they're worried about.

When both are present, your liver produces cocaethylene — a third compound, not present in either substance alone. It has greater cardiotoxicity than MDMA or alcohol individually. It also has a longer half-life, which means it stays in your system after both substances have cleared.

Not more drunk. Chemically different situation.

Know your mix.

**Asset idea:**
Instagram: Clean dark-background graphic. Three elements: MDMA molecule icon + alcohol icon + arrow + cocaethylene. No colour other than amber on the third compound. Label it simply: "what your liver makes." No other copy on the image — full context lives in the caption.

**CTA:**
Know your mix.

**Comments strategy:**
Expect pushback ("aren't they dangerous already?") — don't validate the argument, redirect to mechanism. The point isn't to be scary. The point is cocaethylene is a specific, documentable risk. Stay on the information.

## Day 4 — Thursday

**Platform:** TikTok (primary) / Instagram Stories
**Pillar:** Field Intel
**Brand:** ViceLab

**Hook:**
> You're at hour 9 of a festival. Your friend just went very quiet.

**Full post (TikTok — 50 sec):**
Not sleepy. Not tired. Just... absent.

Eyes open. Still present physically. But the responses are slow, or off, or delayed by a beat too long.

This is one of the presentations of serotonin syndrome — not the dramatic version people think of. Early-stage. Treatable. Easy to miss because it doesn't look like an emergency yet.

What you do in the next 15 minutes matters more than what you do after.

First: temperature. Touch the back of their neck. If they're running hot and sweating more than the environment explains — that's a flag.

Second: agitation or muscle tension in the jaw, neck, or hands. Even mild.

Third: ask them something specific. Not "are you okay?" Something that requires a real answer. "Where did we park?" "What time did we get here?" How long it takes matters.

If two of those three are off, you don't wait for three.

**Asset idea:**
TikTok: Text on screen for each check point as it's spoken. No alarm aesthetic — neutral, clean, matter-of-fact. This is procedural, not scary.
Stories: One-frame per check — temperature / muscle tension / response latency — saved as a highlight under "field intel."

**CTA:**
Watch your crew.

**Comments strategy:**
This post will generate "what do you do after?" — have the response prepared: get them to a cooler space, hydrated if they can swallow, find medical if two flags are present. Keep the reply practical, not alarmist.

## Day 5 — Friday

**Platform:** Instagram / LinkedIn
**Pillar:** Wingmate Protocol
**Brand:** ViceLab

**Hook:**
> "Are you okay?" is the wrong question.

**Full post:**
It gets a yes. Always. Even when the answer is no.

The wingmate check-in works differently. One question, mid-session, specific enough that the answer tells you something real.

Not "are you okay?" — that question has one socially acceptable answer.

Try:
— "When did you last drink water?" (The pause before they answer tells you more than the answer.)
— "You eaten anything since we got here?"
— "How are your hands feeling?"

These land differently. They give permission to say something is off without having to admit it. They bypass the reflex that makes people say they're fine when they're not.

One question. The right one. Mid-session, not when it's already urgent.

That's the protocol.

**Asset idea:**
Instagram: Simple dark card. Three questions listed in the brand typeface. No explanation — the post carries that. The card is the artefact that gets saved and sent.

**CTA:**
Watch your crew.

**Comments strategy:**
Ask: "What check-in question actually works for you?" — treat the replies as real field intelligence. Acknowledge specific ones. This is a genuine community data pull disguised as engagement.

**Full post (TikTok — 40 sec):**
