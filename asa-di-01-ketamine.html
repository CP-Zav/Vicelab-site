Scientific Record

Pharmacological Profile

Class Dissociative Anesthetic NMDA Antagonist Arylcyclohexylamine
Scientific Name 2-(2-Chlorophenyl)-2-(methylamino)cyclohexan-1-one
Common Names Ketamine · Ket · K · Special K · Vitamin K · Super K · Cat Valium
Primary Mechanism Non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonist — open-channel block. The NMDA receptor is a glutamate-gated ion channel that requires both glutamate (binding to NR2 subunit) and glycine (binding to NR1 subunit) to open. Once the channel is open, ketamine enters the pore and physically occludes it from within — blocking calcium ion influx. This "foot-in-the-door" trapping is more persistent than competitive antagonism and accounts for the extended effect duration relative to plasma half-life. Disruption of NMDA-dependent glutamatergic neurotransmission across thalamo-cortical circuits produces the dissociative state.
Secondary Activity κ-opioid partial agonism (contributes to analgesic and dysphoric effects). Sigma-1 receptor activity. HCN1 channel inhibition (relevant to the anesthetic profile). Active metabolite norketamine (~30% potency of parent compound, half-life ~12 hours) contributes to the extended pharmacological window beyond the acute experience.
Half-Life Ketamine: 2 – 3 hours (plasma) · Norketamine: ~12 hours (active metabolite)
Onset Insufflated: 5–15 min · Intramuscular: 3–5 min · Intravenous: ~30 sec · Oral: 15–30 min
Duration Low dose: 45–90 min · High dose (k-hole): 60–120 min · Oral: 2–4 hours · Afterglow / norketamine window: up to 6 hours
Dose Range Threshold (insufflated): ~20–30 mg · Common: 50–100 mg · K-hole: 100–200+ mg (route-dependent)
Typical Effects Dissociative: depersonalisation, derealisation, loss of body awareness, ego dissolution at high doses ("k-hole")
Perceptual: altered spatial perception, visual distortions, tunnel effects, time dilation
Cognitive: memory impairment, slowed processing, confusion, analgesia
Somatic: motor impairment, nausea, mild stimulation at low doses, near-immobility at high doses
Primary Risks Ketamine-Induced Uropathy Bladder Damage Aspiration Risk Psychological Dependence Respiratory Depression (CNS depressants)
Ketamine-induced uropathy — progressive, irreversible urinary tract and bladder damage — is the defining chronic harm of frequent ketamine use and has no equivalent among other recreational substances. At festival doses, aspiration during high-dose immobility is the most immediate life-threatening risk.
Interactions CNS Depressants (alcohol, opioids, benzos) MAOIs Stimulants Tramadol
Combining ketamine with alcohol, opioids, or benzodiazepines compounds respiratory depression from separate routes simultaneously — this is one of the more common mechanisms of dissociative-associated fatality. The combination does not produce a simple additive effect; it is multiplicative at the level of central respiratory drive.

HARM REDUCTION NOTES ASA-DI-01
Evidence-first · Non-judgemental · Operational
Urinary Tract Health
Ketamine-induced uropathy is the defining chronic harm of frequent ketamine use. It is not shared by any other common recreational substance. Damage progresses from urinary tract inflammation to severe bladder contracture requiring surgical intervention — and is largely irreversible once established. Urinary urgency, frequency, pain, or blood in urine are early warning signs. Stop use immediately and seek medical review. This is not a theoretical risk: bladder damage has been documented in recreational users aged 17 to 30 with 1–3 years of regular use.
Frequency and Volume
Ketamine-induced uropathy is dose-cumulative. Daily or near-daily use over months is the characteristic exposure pattern associated with severe bladder damage — but lower-frequency use at high doses also carries risk. There is no established safe frequency. Reducing total use and total dose is the primary protective factor. Weekly use significantly elevates the risk profile compared to occasional use. If the interval between uses is measured in days rather than weeks, frequency is high.
CNS Depressants
Combining ketamine with alcohol, opioids, or benzodiazepines suppresses respiratory drive through multiple independent mechanisms simultaneously. The result is not additive — it is disproportionate. This combination is one of the most common routes to serious ketamine-related harm. The risk is present even at doses of each substance that would be unremarkable alone. If someone using ketamine is also drinking heavily, this warrants active monitoring — not just passive awareness.
Body Position
High-dose ketamine causes near-immobility and commonly causes nausea. Together, these create a real aspiration risk if a person is left on their back. Anyone who is heavily intoxicated and unable to move independently should be placed in the recovery position on their side. Check on them regularly — at high doses they may not be able to call for help. Do not leave anyone alone on their back after a heavy dose.
The K-Hole
A k-hole is a state of profound dissociation occurring at high doses — characterised by ego dissolution, near-total disconnection from the environment, and motor incapacity. For some users this is intentional. Someone in a k-hole is conscious but unresponsive to normal interaction — they are not asleep and should not be left alone. Keep them in the recovery position, warm, monitored, and in a quiet space away from crowds. Do not shake, shout at, or attempt to physically move them unless safety requires it.
Test Your Supply
Ketamine is sometimes substituted with other dissociatives — including PCP, methoxetamine, fluorinated analogues, and novel research chemicals — each with a different and often poorly characterised risk profile. Reagent testing (Mecke and Mandelin reagents) can distinguish ketamine from some common adulterants, but laboratory analysis provides significantly higher confidence. If the onset, intensity, or duration feels different from prior experience, treat it as an adulterant signal.
Wingmate Protocol
Watch your group across the full duration of the experience — including the norketamine tail, which extends several hours beyond the acute phase. Red flags requiring immediate attention: breathing that is very slow, shallow, or irregular; person is unresponsive or cannot be roused; vomiting while on their back or unable to move; skin colour change (pallor, bluish lips or fingertips); temperature that feels very high; rigid posture or unusual muscle tone. Recovery position first. Then get to the event harm reduction or medical team immediately. Tell them what was taken, how much, by what route, and when. Providing this information does not put anyone in legal jeopardy — it enables appropriate care.

Why this plate exists in the archive.

Ketamine exists in a uniquely dual position across clinical and recreational contexts. It is on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines as an anesthetic, has an FDA-approved formulation for treatment-resistant depression (esketamine), and is simultaneously one of the most commonly encountered dissociatives in festival and nightlife settings. This dual life creates a specific information gap: clinical framings focus on therapeutic benefit at controlled doses, while moral or legal framings focus on abuse potential. Neither addresses the practical risk profile of someone using ketamine recreationally in a loud, physical, social environment over a long night.

The harm specific to ketamine — bladder and urinary tract damage — is not present in any other commonly used recreational substance. Most harm reduction knowledge inherited from stimulant, psychedelic, or opioid contexts does not translate. Precise, evidence-based, non-judgmental information that is specific to ketamine's actual risk profile is sparse in the spaces where the substance is most used. This plate exists because the gap between what is widely known about ketamine and what is relevant to an actual user is large enough to matter.

The NMDA open-channel block model was chosen because the mechanism itself is conceptually unusual and distinguishes ketamine from every other entry in the archive. No other plate depicts a substance physically entering an open transmembrane channel and becoming trapped inside. Illustrating this makes two things visually legible: why ketamine's effects persist longer than its plasma half-life would suggest, and why combinations with CNS depressants are so dangerous — they suppress respiratory function through completely separate systems, compounding independently rather than competing for the same receptor site. The fragmented downstream signal represents the disrupted thalamo-cortical communication that is the best current mechanistic account of the dissociative state.
